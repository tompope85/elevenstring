# PRIMER: elevenstring

## What this is for
Let us assume you operate in the world with a set of implicit philosophical commitments - about what counts as true, what kinds of things exist, where you draw the line between yourself and others, what makes a decision the right one. These commitments are usually invisible. Until you encounter a framework, person, AI, or domain that doesn't share them. When that happens, you have three usual options: assimilate the other to your framework (the imperialist move), dismiss the other as wrong (the gatekeeping move), or get confused.
elevenstring is a fourth option: hold your framework as explicitly as you can or choose to prescribe it, encounter others as legitimately different, and engage with them through structured protocols that preserve sovereignty on both sides.
This matters in practice when you're learning a new domain that has its own assumptions; working with someone whose worldview differs from yours; interacting with AI systems whose framings you can't fully verify; navigating regulatory contexts with their own ontologies; tracking how your own positions evolve over time; collaborating across cultures or disciplines; or designing systems that need to coordinate without homogenising.

## The framework distinguishes three layers of commitment:
### The kernel - 
The minimum machinery any framework needs to be a framework at all. elevenstring identifies four primitives: distinction (the ability to draw a this/not-this line), iteration-identity (recognising the same operation when it's applied again), perspective (a vantage from which considerations are individuated), and coherence-constraint (the requirement that the framework's statements fit together enough to be statements). These are universal; they're what makes the framework expressible at all.
### The modules - 
Your specific philosophical commitments. What you think about ontology (what kinds of things exist), mereology (how parts and wholes work), truth (what makes something true), agency (who acts), logic (how inference works), and so on. These are loadable - you can hold particular positions, swap or adjust them when warranted, and track how they change.
### The framings - 
The evaluative lenses you bring across your modules. Coherence (do parts fit?), pragmatic utility (does it work?), care (is this answerable to what matters?), existentialist (does this own its situation?). These are also loadable, and you can stack them. 
The framework grows from these into instance configurations, handshake protocols for inter-agent operation, and a relational atlas of typed encounters with the world.
### The triad
The most distinctive element is the triad - three markers used to type any relation:
### All 
The encountered thing maps cleanly onto your framework. You can subsume it as a configuration of your commitments. Most familiar tools, methodologies, and frameworks within a tradition will type as all.
### Other
The encountered thing is irreducibly different, but you're still in relation with it. You hold it as different rather than assimilating or dismissing. For example - Zen non-dualism, hard determinism, indigenous epistemologies, certain artistic traditions might type as other to a Western analytic framework.
### Nothing 
No relation is possible in this transfer. The thing is genuinely outside engagement.

The triad isn't making metaphysical claims about reality; it's a typology of considerations. A typing of "other" doesn't mean the thing IS irreducibly different in some absolute sense; it means that in your current engagement with it, given your current framework, it's being considered as other.
This matters because the triad gives you a way to engage with what you can't subsume. You don't have to colonise or dismiss a concept (or experience learning or implementing a concept for that matter); you can record the typed relation and proceed in awareness of the difference. Importantly you have a relationship to that difference that you can operationalise for the health of the system interospectively, or communication externally.

## Handshakes
When two agents (humans, AIs, organisations) interact, the framework structures the exchange as a handshake. Each agent publishes a profile - active modules, framings, capabilities, constraints, goal framings, and disclosure permissions. The agents exchange profiles, type the relations between their commitments layer-by-layer using the triad, and from that mapping derive what functional respectful engagement looks like for this pair at this moment.
A human handshaking with an AI knows the AI's processing context, memory horizon, and trained framings. The AI knows the human's affective and temporal constraints. Each can respond appropriately to the other's actual situation rather than to a generic assumption.
Handshakes can fail. A failed handshake isn't a problem to be hidden - it's data to be typed. The framework supports protocols for mediated re-evaluation, information-budgeted disclosure, and explicit typed coexistence when full convergence isn't possible.

## Reviews
The framework updates through observability and the implementation of structured review practices. For example:
- Architectural reviews examine current module commitments and ask whether they're still serving. Quarterly, or as triggered by accumulated friction.
- Metapraxis reviews start from praxis - from what came up in actual doing - and let praxis push back on the current framework. Particularly useful after immersive engagement with a domain.
Each review produces records linking to the evidence supporting any module changes. Significant changes get committed as new version snapshots. The framework grows by trace - nothing gets overwritten, evolutions are visible, and any future query along time / space / perspective coordinates can reconstruct why you held what you held when.
A small example (see [EXAMPLES.md](./EXAMPLES.md) for further examples)
You read a book on Buddhist non-dualism. Your current framework has process ontology, coherence-plus-ownership as a truth criterion, project-oriented agency.
After meaningful exposure, you produce a relation record. Layer by layer:

Ontology: shared rejection of substance, but divergent positive accounts → other with overlap
Truth criterion: yours is articulated propositional, theirs is non-propositional direct realisation → other
Agency: yours presupposes intentional projects; theirs presupposes cessation of project-orientation → other
Aggregate: mostly other at the layers that matter most

You don't have to adopt or reject this position (users might not believe it's possible to adopt or reject a position). But you have a coherent record of what's typed or classified: other, link it to other Buddhist-philosophy relations you've recorded, and proceed in awareness of the difference. If you later begin meditation practice, you can run a metapraxis review afterwards and re-type with experiential rather than studied provenance, creating a new record that links back to this one.
